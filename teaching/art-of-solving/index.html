The Art of Solving Thermodynamics Problems RP's method — a disciplined way to plan your attack

A short video series with a written companion, for second-year Thermofluids. Two chapters, about 20 minutes in total.

One method, not the method. This sets out one way to plan a thermodynamics problem — the way I teach it. It is not the only correct way, and it deliberately shares its foundations with the other worked examples in the unit: the same first law, the same sign convention, the same distinction between state properties and process variables, and the same discipline of stating assumptions before computing. What it adds is a single organiser — the arrow diagram — that makes the plan visible before any number is worked. Use whichever method lets you plan clearly; if this one helps, take it. — RP

The plan, in one line

Everything below is on the worksheet, before a single number is computed.

  1. Identify the ideal gas (Step 0) and its constants from Table A-2.
  2. Find the states and the processes between them.
  3. Draw the arrow diagram — where each property comes from: given, process, or equation of state.
  4. Sketch the P–v diagram the topology implies.
  5. Stage 1 — fix P, v, T at every state.
  6. Stage 2 — internal-energy change, work and heat for every process.
  7. Finish — sum the legs, keeping their signs.
